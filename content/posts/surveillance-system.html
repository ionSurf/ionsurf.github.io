Solar Powered Surveillance System

This solar powered surveillance system is a compilation of many others, and it was the capstone project for the UCSD California IoT Specialization Program through Coursera. It combines the use of embedded systems along with modern telecommunications best practices, data processing, artificial intelligence, electronics and renewable energies. It makes a great example of the power of the Internet of Things by integrating diverse technologies into a grid of systems exchanging data between themselves.

Approach

In order to come up with this project, the following steps were taken:

  1. Research major system components

  2. Research DC components

  3. Purchase system components

  4. Build solar station

  5. Set up DragonBoard 410c

A general diagram laying out all the systems that were going to be connected helped made clear what components were going to be needed per cluster. This also aided into selecting the most affordable and ready-to-use components, since this was meant to be a project that would showcase the program member abilities. it’s important to be careful about compatibility du to the many flavours of solar panels, solar charger controllers, batteries and power inverters, not to mention if you want to add different types of solar panels, fuses, etc.

After a prototype layout was set down and a proper research was made about the components, a Bill Of Materials was done in order to establish a dependency structure and a place of purchase for each one. This list breaks exhibits with great detail all the ingredients needed in this capstone, some of them with notes about crucial information. The majority of the materials here were bought at Amazon for optimizing time and providers constraints.

As the system was put together, hazardous situations were avoided by being careful about polarity, overloading components and weather prone accidents like water leaking or extreme heat. From beginning to end, the panels, the solar charger controller, the battery and the power inverter were placed first. Secondly, the desktop along with the LCD monitor, the keyboard, the mouse, the camera and the lights we put into place. Then, the ESP32 boards along with its sensors were assembled. Next, all the necessary software was installed and the amazon account along with its instances and the GreenGrass Core were setup. Finally, the implementation of AWS “Things” group, the AWS services like lambdas, the DynamoDB database and the information permissions were deployed.

Systems Components

As stated above, a BOM was created to incorporate all the necessary parts for the projects. This list is available here. More information about the most important components will be now exposed.

Solar Panels

For this exercise, one 25W solar panel was used. Nonetheless, some between 10W and 50W could be used. Two 10W solar panels were recommended for the exercise, though it easier to get just one with a little bit more power than those 2 combined, thus having the possibility to evolve into a 50W solar panel system. This panel gives a VOC ( Open Circuit Voltage ) of 21.6V and an ISC ( Short Circuit Current ) of 1.22 A. The original recipe suggested a VOC of 20,6 and an ISC of 1.38 A if connected in parallel. It’s important to mention that the more amperage we have, the faster the batteries will be charged, thus keeping a more stable voltage.

Solar Charge Controller

The solar charge controller has a 20A max output, with USB ports too. It can hold up to 12V or 24V depending on the settings. This is actually a very affordable and good controller for our purposes.

Power Inverter

A standard car power inverter was selected to source the station. It can provide up to 300W, connecting a 12V DC power source and turning it into a 110AC. It comes with a 4.2A Dual USB charging ports.

Battery

The system’s battery is a 12V 20AH one, meaning it can give up to 20A per hour. This must be taken into consideration while thinking about the charge controller and the power inverter, thus making sure that it doesn’t overload by the payload and it doesn’t drain too fast. An easy calculation summing up the approximate power consumed by each element in the system can give you a fair estimate.

Assembly

Solar Energy Grid

Depending on your calculations, solar panels should be set either in parallel for more current or in series for more voltage. For the sake of this exercise, only one will be used, but if two were to be coupled, they’ll definitely be in parallel, thus giving more amps to the controller. Cables should be connected using the ring connectors, that’ll simplify the screwing. If using different cable colors than black and red, make sure you remember which one is the positive and the negative end.

The panels should be attached to the mobile box on the top using screws, keeping together their cables with either tape or a plastic zip belt that should be also tied up to the box. Making a small hole big enough for the cables to go through is also a good idea.

Only one pair of cables should run from the panel grid arrangement to the charge controlled, taking polarity into consideration. Connect the positive cable from the panel grid into the positive input of the charge controller and do the same for the negative end.

The battery, the inverter and the charge controller are meant to live inside the box because that’ll keep them safe and dry. The battery must be connected to the charge controller too, using the positive end for the positive input of the controller and the negative end to the negative probe of the controller. Next, the battery must be connected to the power inverter, tying up positive to positive and negative to negative. It’s very important to know if the battery comes charged or not. Sometimes they do and might give a shock.

After all is set, it’s a good idea to check all connections using a multimeter. This phase of the installation is finished by connecting an outlet to the power inverter.

Remote Desktop

The remote desktop consists of the DragonBoard, the LCD screen, the mouse, the keyboard, the camera, the sensors attached to the mezzanine and the exterior lights. This section is began by connecting the DragonBoard. Attach the LCD screen to the DragonBoard using the HDMI cable, then the keyboard, the mouse and the camera using their respective USB cables. Lastly, connect the board’s AC/DC power converter to the board and then to the power outlet. Press the on button.

Check that the DragonBoard is actually on and that it’s running smoothly in case there’s an OS installed. Otherwise, that’ll be done later. Then, connect the lights to the power outlet and turn them on just to check if they are working properly.

IoT Units

The ESP32 units are meant to be connected with the humidity, temperature, light and sound sensors.
